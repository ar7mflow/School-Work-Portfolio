**Option 1: Amazon**

- This one feels like the obvious high-profile pick right now — Andy Jassy announced in September 2024 that all corporate employees would be required back in the office 5 days a week starting January 2025. There was a huge amount of internal pushback, petitions, and some reported attrition.
- Framing-wise, Jassy explicitly tied the mandate to "collaboration," "innovation," and protecting Amazon's culture — which lines up with what Chapter 7 talks about in terms of how problems and opportunities get framed by leadership (and who controls that framing).
- **Structure:** Amazon is a divisional setup — AWS, retail, advertising, devices — each basically a business within the business. Centralization is high at the top (the S-team), and the company is known for being heavily formalized through its processes and written memos. These dimensions all connect to what Chapter 13 covers.
- **Culture:** Amazon runs on its 16 Leadership Principles — "Customer Obsession," "Bias for Action," "Frugality," "Deliver Results," etc. The culture is widely described as demanding and performance-driven, and the RTO rollout fits that narrative. The LP artifacts (the posters, the mechanisms built around them, how they shape hiring) give us a lot to point to in cultural artifact terms — ties into Chapter 14.
- There's a ton of public information on all three dimensions (RTO, structure, culture), so I don't think we'd have trouble finding sources.

---

**Option 2: Google (Alphabet)**

- Google has been enforcing hybrid RTO since 2023 — 3 mandatory days in-office, with badge-swipe tracking added in, which caused its own controversy. Layoffs also happened in the same period as stricter attendance rules, which created a lot of tension.
- **Structure:** Alphabet runs more as a matrix — a parent holding multiple business units (Google Search, YouTube, Google Cloud, DeepMind, etc.) with functional divisions cutting across them. Has historically felt more decentralized than Amazon, though that's reportedly changed under Sundar Pichai. Chapter 13 stuff on centralization vs. decentralization maps onto this pretty directly.
- **Culture:** This one is actually interesting because of the *tension* — Google's whole brand identity is built around openness, innovation, psychological safety, employee autonomy, and all the perks (the free food, the open offices, the "20% time"). Those perks are basically artifacts that were built to get people in the office voluntarily, so mandating that same thing creates a real contradiction between stated values and actual policy. Chapter 14 on culture layers (artifacts vs. underlying values/assumptions) would be useful here.
- Might be good for a richer cultural analysis specifically, but there's slightly less concrete structural info available compared to Amazon.

---

**Quick take:**

- I'd probably lean toward Amazon if we want something with clear-cut, well-documented info across all three dimensions — and the RTO story is still actively unfolding.
- Google is a strong backup, especially if we want to dig into culture-policy tension.
