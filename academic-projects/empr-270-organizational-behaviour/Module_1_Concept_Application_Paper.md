## Context

During the summer of 2025, I worked as a shift supervisor at a local logistics company coordinating warehouse operations. Our team was tasked with implementing a new inventory management system before the fall rush, and my manager asked me to select two employees from our twelve-person crew to lead the training sessions for other staff members. I immediately thought of Marcus, who had been with the company for eight months and always showed up looking professional—he wore clean work attire, kept his station organized, and regularly offered suggestions during team meetings. I barely considered the other candidates because Marcus seemed like the obvious choice. For the second position, I picked Sarah, primarily because she and Marcus seemed to get along well during lunch breaks, and I figured they'd work well together.

The training rollout was a disaster. Marcus struggled to explain the software's technical aspects and became visibly frustrated when employees asked detailed questions he couldn't answer. Sarah, meanwhile, missed two of the scheduled training sessions entirely, citing personal conflicts that came up last minute. The project fell behind by three weeks, and several employees had to be retrained. After the situation was resolved with help from other staff, I discovered that Jenny, a quieter team member I'd barely noticed, had actually used similar inventory software at her previous job and had been hoping to volunteer but never felt comfortable speaking up. The whole experience forced me to reflect on how I'd made such a poor leadership decision.

## Analysis

Looking back at this experience through the lens of organizational behaviour concepts, I realize my decision-making was heavily compromised by multiple perceptual biases that distorted my assessment of my team members' actual capabilities. These systematic errors in judgment not only led to immediate project delays but also demonstrated how unconscious cognitive shortcuts can undermine effective organizational decision-making.

The most prominent bias affecting my judgment was the **halo effect**, a perceptual distortion where an impression formed in one area influences our opinion in other areas. According to course material, the halo effect occurs when we allow a single positive (or negative) trait to create an overall impression that colors our entire perception of an individual. In Marcus's case, his professional appearance and visible participation in meetings created a positive halo that led me to assume he possessed strong technical skills and training abilities—qualities I never actually verified. His neat workspace made me unconsciously attribute to him characteristics like competence, reliability, and expertise, even though these traits had nothing to do with his actual knowledge of inventory management systems. This cognitive shortcut prevented me from conducting a more thorough assessment of who was genuinely qualified for the technical training role.

Further compounding this error was **confirmation bias**, the tendency to seek out and interpret information in ways that confirm our pre-existing beliefs while dismissing contradictory evidence. Once I'd mentally decided Marcus was the ideal candidate based on my initial positive impression, I unconsciously looked for evidence that supported this conclusion. I recalled moments when he'd made suggestions during meetings as proof of his leadership potential, while completely overlooking signs that should have raised concerns—like the fact that his suggestions were usually about scheduling and logistics, never about technical processes. Meanwhile, I'd developed a mental schema about Jenny as "quiet" and "reserved," which led me to interpret her silence as disinterest or lack of capability rather than considering alternative explanations like conscientiousness, introversion, or simply not wanting to interrupt others. My confirmation bias created a self-reinforcing loop where I only paid attention to information that validated my initial, flawed assessment.

The **fundamental attribution error** also played a significant role in how this situation unfolded and how I initially interpreted its failure. This bias refers to our tendency to attribute other people's behavior to internal, dispositional factors while underestimating the influence of external, situational factors. When Sarah missed those training sessions, my immediate reaction was to attribute her absence to character flaws—I thought she was irresponsible or lacked commitment to the project. I completely failed to consider situational factors that might have been at play, such as family emergencies, scheduling conflicts created by the company's irregular shift patterns, or even the possibility that she felt unprepared for the role and didn't know how to communicate that concern to me. Similarly, when Marcus struggled during training sessions, I initially attributed his difficulties to nervousness or lack of confidence rather than recognizing the situational reality: he simply didn't have the technical background required for the task, which was entirely my fault for not properly assessing his qualifications beforehand.

What makes this experience particularly instructive from an organizational behaviour perspective is how these three biases interacted and reinforced each other, creating a cascading series of poor decisions. The halo effect created my initial misperception, confirmation bias prevented me from correcting it even when contradictory information was available, and the fundamental attribution error led me to blame individuals for failures that were actually caused by my own flawed selection process. If I had applied evidence-based decision-making practices—such as reviewing employees' prior experience, conducting brief interviews about their familiarity with similar systems, or simply asking who felt confident in their technical abilities—I would have discovered Jenny's relevant background and likely avoided the entire problematic situation.

This experience has fundamentally changed how I approach team selection and performance evaluation. I now understand that our perceptions are not objective reality but rather mental constructions shaped by cognitive shortcuts that, while efficient, can lead to systematically flawed judgments. Recognizing these biases doesn't eliminate them entirely, but awareness creates the opportunity to implement deliberate strategies—like standardized evaluation criteria, seeking input from multiple sources, and consciously challenging initial impressions—that can counteract their influence and lead to better organizational outcomes.
